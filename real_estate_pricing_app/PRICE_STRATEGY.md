# Setting the initial price
The initial price is determined through a process called **Comparative Market Analysis (CMA)**. The goal is to triangulate "Fair Market Value" by balancing historical data with current competitive positioning.

Here is the professional framework for setting an initial price, categorized by the data features you should include:

### 1. The "Big Three" Comparables (Comps)
Agents don't look at all homes; they filter for the most predictive "Comps" using a specific hierarchy:
* **Sold Comps (Past 3–6 Months):** This is the "Anchor." It represents what buyers were actually willing to pay. 
    * *Algorithm Rule:* Weight these at 60% of your initial estimate. Use a "Recency Decay" function—a sale from 2 weeks ago is significantly more predictive than one from 5 months ago.
* **Pending Comps (Under Contract):** This represents the *current* market clearing price. While the final price is hidden until closing, the "Days on Market" for a pending sale tells you if the list price was "hot" or "cold."
    * *Algorithm Rule:* Weight these at 30%. If a pending home went under contract in < 7 days, the market is moving faster than the "Sold" data suggests.
* **Active Comps (The Competition):** This is what the seller is fighting against today.
    * *Algorithm Rule:* Weight these at 10%. Use them to set the "Upper Bound." You rarely want to be the most expensive active listing in a 1-mile radius unless the property is significantly superior.

### 2. Standardized Adjustments (The "Grid")
Agents use a "Value Grid" to normalize different properties to the subject home. You should apply these +/- adjustments to the baseline price:
* **Square Footage:** Usually adjusted at 50% of the "Price per Square Foot" of the neighborhood (e.g., if the neighborhood average is \$300/sqft, add/subtract \$150 for every foot of difference).
* **Bedroom/Bathroom Counts:** A 4th bedroom or 3rd bathroom typically adds a fixed "chunk" of value (e.g., +\$15k–\$25k) rather than a linear square footage increase.
* **Lot/View Premium:** Subjective but critical. Professional agents look for "External Obsolescence" (backing into a highway) or "Quiet Cul-de-sacs."

### 3. Psychological "Bracket" Pricing
This is the most common mistake FSBO sellers make. Professionals price for **Search Filters**, not for a specific "round" number they want to net.
* **The Filter Rule:** Most buyer portals (Zillow, Redfin, MLS) use \$25,000 or \$50,000 increments. 
    * *Agent Logic:* Pricing a home at **\$505,000** is a disaster because you lose every buyer with a "\$500k Max" filter. Pricing at **\$499,000** or exactly **\$500,000** captures both the "\$450k–\$500k" and the "\$500k–\$550k" buyer pools.
* **The "99" Strategy:** While retail uses \$9.99, real estate often uses "Value Pricing" (e.g., exactly \$500,000) to appear at the top of both "High-to-Low" and "Low-to-High" searches.

### 4. Market Velocity Multipliers (The "Absorption Rate")
Agents adjust the initial price based on inventory levels:
* **Seller's Market (< 3 months of inventory):** Price at or 2–3% *above* the most recent sold comp.
* **Buyer's Market (> 6 months of inventory):** Price 3–5% *below* the most recent sold comp to "undercut" the competition and exit the market quickly.

### 5. The "FSBO Premium" Trap
When determining an initial price for a FSBO property, your must account for **Commission Savings**. 
* **The Logic:** A FSBO seller often thinks: *"I'm saving 6% on commission, so I can price 6% higher."* * **The Reality:** Buyers looking at FSBOs *also* know the seller is saving commission and expect to share that saving. 
* **Professional Recommendation:** Price the FSBO at **98% of Fair Market Value**. This creates a "Deal" perception that drives multiple offers, which usually pushes the final price back up to 100% or higher.

### Summary for your Initial Pricing Function:
1.  **Calculate Baseline:** Weighted average of 3–5 closest "Sold" comps (last 180 days).
2.  **Apply Feature Offsets:** Adjust for beds, baths, and sqft deltas.
3.  **Apply Velocity Buffer:** +/- % based on current neighborhood Absorption Rate.
4.  **Snap to Bracket:** Round the result to the nearest major search filter (e.g., \$475k, \$500k).
5.  **FSBO Adjustment:** Subtract 1–2% to ensure the "No-Agent" listing doesn't sit stale.

# Adjusting the price
Professional real estate agents approach pricing as a dynamic feedback loop. You should distinguish between **Time-Based Triggers** (how long it's been listed) and **Engagement-Based Triggers** (showings and digital activity).

In the 2026 market, where the national median Days on Market (DOM) is approximately **66 days**, the following "professional logic" is standard:

### 1. The Adjustment Direction
* **Downward (95% of cases):** To find the "market clearing price."
* **Upward (5% of cases):** Used strategically for "Bracket Hopping." If a home is at \$495,000, moving it to \$505,000 might seem counterintuitive, but it places the home in front of a new, higher-budget search filter ($500k+) where it may appear as a "bargain" compared to \$600k listings.

### 2. Frequency & Timing (The "Rule of Three")
Agents typically follow a **21-day review cycle**. The first three weeks are the "Golden Window" of peak visibility.

| Phase | Duration | Professional Action |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Launch Phase** | Days 1–14 | No adjustments. Monitor "Saves" and "Showings." |
| **First Review** | Day 21 | If < 10 showings and 0 offers: **3–5% reduction.** |
| **Second Review** | Day 45 | If < 5 showings in last 14 days: **Another 5% reduction.** |
| **Pivot Phase** | Day 60+ | Total strategy overhaul (re-shoot photos, pull and re-list, or 10%+ drop). |

### 3. Engagement-Based Trigger Logic (Algorithm Inputs)
If you are mining data, these specific thresholds are the "hard signals" agents use to justify a price drop to a seller:

#### **Signal: Showings vs. Offers**
* **10+ Showings / 0 Offers:** The home is "showing well but priced high."
    * *Adjustment:* **3–5% reduction.** The price is likely within 5% of market value, but buyers are finding better value elsewhere.
* **< 3 Showings in 14 Days:** The home is "dead on arrival."
    * *Adjustment:* **6–10% reduction.** This suggests the property isn't even making the "shortlist" for tours.
* **0 Showings / High Views:** The "Photo Gap."
    * *Adjustment:* Improve assets or **10% reduction.** The digital presentation is enticing, but the price tag is an immediate disqualifier.

#### **Signal: The "Zillow/Redfin Save" Velocity**
* **High Saves / 0 Showings:** Buyers are "waiting for the drop." This is a classic signal that the market has collectively decided your price is too high and is tracking the listing until it hits a lower threshold.

### 4. FSBO-Specific issues
Since you are targeting **For Sale By Owner**, your should account for these "FSBO Biases" which differ from agent listings:
* **The Lag Factor:** FSBO sellers typically wait **2x longer** than agents to make their first price cut because of emotional anchoring.
* **"Chasing the Market":** FSBOs often make small, incremental cuts (e.g., \$1,000 or \$5,000 on a \$500k home). Professionals consider this a failure because it doesn't cross the **Search Bracket Thresholds** (e.g., \$450k, \$475k, \$500k).
* **The Stale Stigma:** FSBO listings that hit 90+ days without a cut are often perceived by buyers as having "hidden defects," leading to "lowball" offers 15–20% below list.

### Recommendations
You should prioritize **Crossing the Bracket**. 
> **Algorithm Rule:** If a reduction is triggered, the new price should be `Max(Suggested_Drop, Next_Lowest_Search_Filter)`. For example, if your model suggests a 4% drop on a \$512,000 home, the algorithm should output **\$499,000** to catch everyone with a \$500k max filter.
